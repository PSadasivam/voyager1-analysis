About Prabhu's Deep Space Labs

Prabhu's Deep Space Labs (PDSL) is not a recent initiative — it is a lifelong pursuit that began during my undergraduate years in Physics in the early 1990s. That was when the first questions took hold: How does the universe truly work? What lies beyond what we can observe? Those questions never left.

Over time, my professional path evolved into building large-scale technology platforms and data systems. But the curiosity for the cosmos remained constant. PDSL is where these two worlds come together — physics and data science, curiosity and computation.

At its foundation, PDSL is a personal research platform focused on exploring deep space through real scientific data and disciplined analysis.

The journey began with NASA's Voyager 1 — humanity's farthest-reaching spacecraft, now traveling through interstellar space. Using datasets from NASA's Space Physics Data Facility (SPDF) and JPL HORIZONS, I developed an end-to-end analysis pipeline to interpret magnetic field variations, plasma wave activity, and heliospheric transitions.

Building on this, the research expanded into interstellar object analysis. Current work includes the study of 3I/ATLAS — focusing on its trajectory, observational data, and implications for interstellar material.

In parallel, I am developing a scientific hypothesis exploring whether our universe itself could exist within a black hole. This builds on General Relativity and foundational black hole physics. The paper is available here: Universe Inside a Black Hole — Hypothesis Paper.

From Deep Space to Enterprise Platforms

My undergraduate degree was in Physics, and astronomy has always been a passion of mine.

When Voyager 1 launched, I was a child. Today, it has traveled more than 15 billion kilometers from Earth and continues to transmit data — decades beyond its original mission life.

What fascinates me is not just the distance, but the design philosophy behind it.

That spacecraft was engineered for a future no one could fully predict. It had to operate with extreme precision and reliability, without the assumption of constant human intervention. The engineers had to think decades ahead — designing for autonomy, resilience, and clarity of purpose.

That mindset has deeply influenced how I approach technology leadership.

When I assumed leadership of Healthcare Technology, I stepped back to understand the landscape — the structural challenges, growth opportunities, and competitive dynamics. Because if we are not clear on the “why” and the “what,” the “how” becomes reactive.

That reflection enabled us to define a one-platform vision — cohesive, scalable, and aligned to long-term direction.

Voyager reinforces a second principle: the systems we design today will outlive immediate roadmaps. Platforms, data models, and architecture decisions may operate for 10–15 years. Architectural integrity and disciplined engineering are compounding investments.

Great engineering is not about building systems that run forever. It is about building systems that deliver value reliably for far longer than expected.

Leadership is the same. It is about designing platforms, teams, and organizations that endure — not just for the next sprint, but for the long horizon.

3I/ATLAS: Interstellar Comet Research

Orbital Mechanics

The most hyperbolic trajectory ever observed (e = 6.139) with a nearly retrograde inclination of 175.1°. Perihelion at 1.356 AU confirms unambiguous interstellar origin — a visitor from another star system.

JWST Volatile Detection

First direct spectroscopic measurement of H₂O, CO₂, and CO in an interstellar comet’s coma. These detections constrain ice chemistry in an extrasolar protoplanetary disk.
